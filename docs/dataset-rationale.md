# Dataset Rationale

This document records why the Thirsty Machines project relies on five specific public datasets, what columns the project depends on within each, what their known limitations are, and how they join into a single analytical surface. Source URLs, access dates, licensing terms, and any preprocessing applied at ingestion are documented per-dataset in `data/raw/<source>/PROVENANCE.md` rather than repeated here. The five datasets, in the order the integration notebook reads them, are: the Jegham per-query inference benchmark, Epoch AI's machine-learning models database, Ember's electricity data, the IM3 Open Source Data Center Atlas, and the World Resources Institute's Aqueduct 4.0 water-risk indicators.

## Jegham per-query inference benchmark

Jegham et al.'s "How Hungry is AI?" benchmark is the project's spine because it is the only public dataset that estimates per-query energy, water, and carbon for a wide cohort of frontier large language models in a methodologically uniform way. It was chosen over Luccioni's "Power Hungry Processing" study, which covers fewer models and predates the GPT-5 and Claude 4 generations, and over individual provider sustainability disclosures, which never report competitor models. The project consumes the snapshot file `DataSnapshotOct26.csv`, which contains 66 models across three query lengths (short, medium, long), with mean and standard-deviation columns for combined energy in watt-hours, combined water in millilitres at both site and source, and combined carbon in grams of CO₂ equivalent. The snapshot also publishes the inferred infrastructure parameters (PUE, WUE, CIF) that drive its calculations, and pre-computes aggregate footprints at one-billion, fifty-billion, and one-hundred-billion query scales with everyday equivalences. Alongside the frozen paper-vintage snapshot, the project also ingests Jegham's current live snapshot (`artificialanalysis_environmental.csv`) and monthly time-series footprint (`Monthly_LLM_Environmental_Footprint.csv`) for analyses requiring up-to-date or temporal-trend views, and the AWS and Microsoft per-region infrastructure-multiplier files that supply the PUE, WUE, and CIF values used in the geographic join; the upstream Artificial Analysis benchmark inputs (`artificialanalysis_clean{short,medium,long}.csv`) ship in the same Jegham bundle but are not ingested, as they are pre-environmental-model inputs whose effects are already incorporated into the three environmental-footprint files above. The dataset's central limitation is that the proprietary providers do not publish their per-query energy directly; Jegham infers infrastructure from public API performance and disclosed multipliers, and confidence intervals are wide and asymmetric. The project flags this throughout. Jegham joins to the rest of the system through its per-model `Host` and `Company` columns (linking to data-centre geography), through per-region multiplier files for AWS and Microsoft (linking to grid intensity), and through model identity (linking to Epoch AI's training metadata).

## Epoch AI models database

Epoch AI provides the longest and most schema-complete historical record of large machine-learning systems, covering 3,509 models with publication dates, training compute in floating-point operations, parameter counts, training hardware, and — for the post-2023 frontier subset that matters for this project — direct power-draw figures. It was chosen over the Stanford AI Index, which is less granular and less frequently updated, and over Papers With Code, which has no power-draw column at all. The project depends on `Model`, `Publication date`, `Organization`, `Training compute (FLOP)`, `Parameters`, `Training hardware`, and `Training power draw (W)`. Coverage on the power-draw column is partial — populated for 481 of 2,076 post-2023 models and for 39 of 137 flagged frontier models — but the populated subset includes the named systems any non-technical reader will recognise (GPT-4, Llama 3.1-405B, Grok 3, Gemini 1.0 Ultra, PaLM, Gopher), which is exactly what the project's training-side trajectory chart needs. The dataset's limitation is the partial power-draw coverage; the project handles this by labelling the chart as "frontier models with disclosed training power" rather than implying exhaustive coverage. Epoch joins to Jegham via model name and to nothing else; its role is to provide training-side temporal context that Jegham, an inference-only benchmark, cannot.

## Ember electricity data

Ember publishes the cleanest open dataset of national and US-state electricity generation and emissions, updated twice monthly, with carbon intensity reported directly in grams of CO₂ per kilowatt-hour — the exact unit needed to convert model energy consumption into geographic carbon footprints. It was chosen over the IEA, whose granular country data sits behind a paywall, and over Our World in Data, which is itself a mirror of Ember and is treated as a fallback access path. The project depends on the `Variable == "CO2 intensity"` rows, with `gCO2/kWh` units, joined on country (yearly file, 228 countries) and US state (US states file, all 50 states plus DC). Latest year of coverage is 2025. The dataset's limitation is that emissions intensity is reported at country and state granularity but not at the sub-state grid-zone level where individual data centres physically draw power; the project documents this and uses state-level intensity as a defensible proxy. Ember joins to Jegham through the `Host` column's region attribution, to the IM3 Atlas through US state names, and to Aqueduct through country names.

## IM3 Open Source Data Center Atlas

The IM3 Atlas, published by Pacific Northwest National Laboratory in February 2026, geocodes 1,479 US data-centre facilities derived from OpenStreetMap with operator, county, square footage, latitude, and longitude. It was chosen over Data Center Map (commercial, no public API), over the AI Data Center Index (smaller scope), and over DC Byte (paywalled). The project consumes the campus layer — 135 facilities with sqft and coordinates fully populated — for the geographic visualisation, supplemented by the buildings layer for operator-attribution lookups. The columns used are `name`, `operator`, `state`, `county`, `sqft`, `lat`, `lon`, and `type`. Two limitations matter: the dataset reports facility footprint in square feet rather than electrical capacity in megawatts, so the map encodes facility size as footprint rather than capacity; and operator attribution is sparse on smaller facilities (57.8% on the campus layer), though most unattributed major facilities can be back-filled by parsing the `name` field. The IM3 Atlas joins to Ember through US state names and to Aqueduct through US state names mapped to the Aqueduct province layer.

## WRI Aqueduct 4.0

The World Resources Institute's Aqueduct 4.0 publishes the most widely cited country and sub-national water-risk indicators in open form, with baseline water stress scored on a 0–5 scale. It was chosen over WaterGAP (academic, less accessible) and over FAO AQUASTAT (country-only annual aggregates without the indicator family Aqueduct provides). The project consumes the `country_baseline` and `province_baseline` sheets, filtered to `indicator_name == "bws"` and `weight == "Tot"`, and depends on the `score`, `cat`, and `label` columns. Province-level coverage includes all 51 US states and territories, which gives the geographic visualisation state-level water-stress overlay rather than country-level only. The dataset's known gap is Singapore, which Aqueduct cannot score under its hydrological-basin methodology; this is documented as a known limitation. Aqueduct joins to Ember through country name and to the IM3 Atlas through US state name.

## Integration

The five datasets join into a single analytical surface keyed on three identifiers: model name (Jegham × Epoch), country name (Ember × Aqueduct), and US state name (Ember × IM3 × Aqueduct). The integration produces, for any given query routed to any given cloud region, a per-query environmental footprint with three independent geographic context layers. As a concrete example: Virginia hosts 319 of the 1,479 US data centres in the IM3 Atlas, has a 2025 grid carbon intensity of 327 gCO₂/kWh in Ember, and a baseline water stress score of 2.56 in Aqueduct — versus Washington's 82 facilities, 124 gCO₂/kWh intensity, and 1.20 water stress score. The same workload running in Virginia carries roughly 2.6× the carbon footprint and meaningfully higher water-supply pressure than the same workload in Washington. This kind of cross-dataset triangulation is the analytical claim the project is built to make, and the integration of the five datasets is what enables it.

For licensing terms, exact source URLs, access dates, and any per-dataset preprocessing applied at ingestion, see the `PROVENANCE.md` file in each `data/raw/<source>/` directory.
